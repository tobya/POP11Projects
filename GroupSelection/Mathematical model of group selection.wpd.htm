Group Selection:

 

Introduction:

 

One of the puzzles of evolutionary theory is the fact that many natural animal populations seem to exhibit a great degree of cooperation.  How can cooperation come about if evolution is solely driven on survival of the fittest, and the assumption that if the payoff for individual behaviour is greater than cooperation, which it usually is, then how could cooperation evolve.  One of the main theories is that of group selection[1].

 

Evolution in two ways at once.

 

In a traditional evolutionary model, there is only one direction of evolution.  This is the direction of individual selection, the fittest individual gets to produce more offspring and the population moves in that direction.  However, imagine if the individual lived in a society of creatures which had to collect a certain quantity of food before winter arrived, if an individual is mating instead of collecting food, then the colony as a whole could be wiped out.  As a result it is likely that all his efforts to reproduce will be lost when the colony dies.  So even though individual selection favours those that mate more, group selection favours those colonies that collect enough food, so a balance will evolve where individuals in the group mate as much as possible, but still collect enough food to survive the winter.  They cooperate.

Model:

Consider a metapopulation of organisms separated into geographically sparse populations with some interaction through migration and recolonization.  Each genotype consists of a fixed number of  loci.  There is a possibility of two Alleles for each locus.  For simplicity we will call the Loci A, B, C, D ... etc and the two possible alleles for each loci 0 or 1.  This gives us binary notation. 

 

The population of each group is at least 2 greater than the highest binary value (HighBinary) generated by the genotype (currently I am using 15 greater).  This is to ensure that there is always a mating pair left at the end of individual selection.

 

The life cycle proceeds as follows.  Individual selection is followed by group selection, flow migration, recolonization, reproduction and mutation.  Then the cycle begins again.  This is a non discrete population model, but could be modified to show discrete populations.

 

Individual selection is calculated in the following manner.  A selection value is generated for each individual within a group dependant upon its genotype. Selection value is dependant on binary notation of Genotype structure.  Once this selection value is found this can represent the phenotype. 

One phenotype is then chosen randomly from those with the highest selection value, ie., of all the high value phenotype only one gets to eat.  The chosen phenotype then (because its tough and strong) gets to eat (or shoot in the killer crazed cowboy problem) a number of other phenotypes within its group and the larger it is the more it has to eat, in fact as many as its phenotype value.  Since the other phenotypes within the group are varying strengths, it is more difficult to eat some, so their chances of being eaten by the strongest phenotype are proportional to their phenotype.  Then each group is recorded along with what phenotypes were eaten, which are alive, and how many are alive.

 

Group selection now takes place.  The group selection value is calculated simply as the number of organisms still alive within the group.  Then either a fixed number of groups is replaced, with a probability inversely proportional to their group selection value  (ie., the greater the  selection value the less likely they are to be extinguished) this is currently being used. Another method is for  each group to have a probability of being replaced  (Pg ) of :

 

Pg=  1 - GroupSelectionValue / HighBinary   

 

Pg = 1 - GS / HB

 

 

Migration now happens.  For each individual within a group there is a chance, ‘the migration probability’ that they will migrate.  If IslandMigration is true then each migrant is treated individually, or else migrants from the same group migrate to the same new group.  Migrants are put into a randomly selected group including recently extinguished groups.

 

 

 

Once the migrant pool has been created and flow migration has happened, recolonization of extinguished groups takes place.  Extinguished groups are recolonised by a mechanism based on type of recolonization.  Recolonizers are chosen from groups in a similar manner to migrants (using ‘the recolonization probability’), but migrants cannot be chosen as recolonists.  Once chosen if IslandRecolonization is true then each colonist is treated individually and placed individually however if false then each group of colonists from the same original group are placed in the same colony.  To place colonists,  a previously extinguished colony is chosen and one or more colonists are placed in it.  To place the next colonist, another extinguished colony is chosen, previously colonised groups can only accept more colonists if all previously extinguished groups have already been colonised, this ensures that extinguished groups are recolonised.

 

 

Once all previously extinguished groups have been recolonized reproduction takes place. Reproduction increases the population of both newly colonized and old groups back to the constant population size being used for the run.  This is done using the genetic algorithm technique of sexual reproduction using selection and crossover.  The GA ‘Fitness’ of each organism in the group is equal to their previously found selection value.

 

Mutation now takes place.  A simple operator checks each Locus in each Genotype in each group and flips the allele with a specific prespecified probability.

 

Thats it really, one hopes that the group selection will over time reduce the individual selection value, leading to less dying in groups leading to leaner organisms and greater world harmony.

This model is a strange variation on the ‘Killer Crazed Cowboy’ problem posed as a problem for this assignment.  However no grid is used and there is no ‘line of sight’ or barricades etc.  Instead there is a simple value assigned to a genotype representing its binary value (currently 24),this is the number of other organisms the fittest gets to shoot (or eat) in its group.  There are several groups in the overall ‘meta’ population, each with an identical number of organisms.  Individual selection is set up so as it favours individuals with higher values, but group selection is set up to favour lower values. 

 

If the various variables (mutation, migration and colonization) are set properly group selection should be a stronger force and reduce the value of the fittest genotype in a group (thereby the number killed in each group).  This can sort of be seen as the evolution of cooperation for the common good of the whole group.  I have put down a more detailed model below.  This is the model that I have implemented.
